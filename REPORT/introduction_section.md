# Introduction

Bacterial infections remain a significant cause of mortality worldwide, with the burden being particularly high in developing countries like India. The rapid and accurate identification of pathogenic bacteria is crucial for effective treatment and in curbing the growing threat of antimicrobial resistance. Gram staining which is a century-old technique is often the first and most critical step in this process, differentiating bacteria into broad categories based on their cell wall characteristics. This initial classification into Gram-positive/negative and cocci/bacilli guides clinicians in making timely and informed decisions about antibiotic therapy.

The conventional approach of microscopic examination of Gram-stained slides presents certain difficulties. The accuracy of result interpretation is significantly influenced by the microbiologist's expertise and experience, thereby contributing to considerable inter-observer variability. This manual procedure is also extremly labor-intensive and time-consuming, potentially creating a significant impediment in environments with higher sample throughput or a lack of adequately trained staff. Moreover, the presence of substandard sample quality, staining artifacts, and nuanced morphological distinctions among bacterial species can complicate precise classification, even for seasoned professionals.

In recent years, the application of artificial intelligence, specifically deep learning, has gained traction in the automation and standardization of medical image analysis. Numerous investigations have highlighted the efficacy of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) in the precise classification of bacterial images. For example, Kogilavani et al. [1] employed a bespoke CNN model for bacterial image classification, achieving an impressive 98% accuracy. Likewise, Çınar and Yildirim [2] formulated a deep learning model for the classification of Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, with a reported accuracy of 96%. Despite these encouraging findings, these studies frequently encounter constraints, such as reliance on limited, often highly curated and clean datasets, which may not accurately represent the intricacies of actual clinical samples. Furthermore, many extant models prioritize binary classification (Gram-positive versus Gram-negative), thereby offering less detailed information than is frequently required for clinical decision-making.

This study aims to address these gaps by developing a robust deep learning pipeline for the automated, four-class classification of bacteria from Gram-stained microscopic images: Gram-Positive Cocci (GPC), Gram-Positive Bacilli (GPB), Gram-Negative Cocci (GNC), and Gram-Negative Bacilli (GNB). We move beyond traditional CNN architectures to explore more advanced models, including Vision Transformers (ViT) [3] and the VOLO architecture [4], which have shown state-of-the-art performance in various computer vision tasks.

The novelty of our approach lies in the comprehensive four-class classification system and the application of a cutting-edge model architecture to this specific problem. We also employ a dataset that includes a diverse range of clinical samples and utilize data augmentation techniques, including the generation of synthetic images, to enhance the model's robustness and generalization capabilities.

The successful application of this system could significantly impact clinical microbiology. Our model provides a quick, accurate, and standardized method for initially identifying bacteria, could be a valuable tool for microbiologists. This would reduce the time needed for results and improve workflow. In settings with limited resources, such a tool could help overcome a lack of expertise, allowing for faster diagnoses and more targeted treatments. This, in turn, would lead to better patient outcomes and help combat antimicrobial resistance.
