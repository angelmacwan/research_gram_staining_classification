Bacterial keratitis is a serious, sight-threatening infection of the cornea that can result in corneal scarring, perforation, endophthalmitis, and ultimately irreversible blindness. A defining clinical characteristic of bacterial keratitis is its potential for rapid and aggressive progression, with certain highly virulent bacterial pathogens capable of causing rapid and severe corneal destruction over a short time frame if not promptly treated [1]. However, accurate and rapid classification of bacteria into Gram-positive and Gram-negative cocci and bacilli is a cornerstone of clinical microbiology, as it directly influences antimicrobial selection, disease severity assessment, and clinical outcomes [2, 3].

Recent advancements in artificial intelligence have increasingly been applied to automate the interpretation of microbiological images, aiming to reduce manual workload and human error. Several recent studies from 2022 to 2025 have explored machine learning and deep learning methodologies, including convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and Vision Transformers (ViTs), for the classification of Gram-stained smears [4, 5]. Comparative analyses of these algorithms have demonstrated the feasibility of automated microbial classification [6], with various approaches employing tailored image processing pipelines to differentiate between Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria [7, 8]. Despite these strides, existing solutions frequently face limitations such as reliance on computationally heavy architectures, insufficient generalization across diverse clinical datasets, or a lack of comprehensive architectural benchmarking. Furthermore, many prior models fail to strike an optimal balance between robust predictive accuracy and computational efficiency. Our study builds upon these foundational works but addresses their limitations by rigorously evaluating a broad spectrum of current vision architectures to identify a highly optimized model that delivers superior classification accuracy with a lighter computational footprint, making it exceptionally well-suited for rapid, real-time diagnostic implementation.

Our research addresses this gap by leveraging transfer learning–based methods on Gram-stained microscopy images to achieve accurate and precise classification of bacterial morphology and Gram characteristics. By utilizing pre-trained deep learning models, transfer learning substantially reduces the dependence on large, expertly annotated datasets, which remains a major challenge in microbiological imaging studies. We implemented a rigorous model selection and validation framework by systematically evaluating 24 state-of-the-art deep learning architectures, encompassing diverse convolutional, transformer-based, and hybrid models, followed by fine-tuning of the top-performing architecture and robust cross-validation. This comprehensive and reproducible protocol delivers rapid, accurate, and reliable bacterial classification, offering a practical decision-support tool for microbiologists that can improve diagnostic consistency and streamline laboratory workflows. Importantly, such an automated system has strong potential utility in resource-limited settings, where limited access to trained expertise necessitates faster and dependable diagnostic solutions.
